VISUALIZING DATA WITH TABLEAU

BY BLACK BOARD LEARNING
Getting started – Connecting to your data

Tableau supports a wide range of data sources. You can connect to any source from the Tableau homepage, ranging from spreadsheets to cloud database in order to import your data. After you connect to the data, you will be able to see two tabs that you can see at the bottom – one is the data source, and the other will be your sheets tab.

The data source page shows the sheets or tables in your data. You can drag the required table to the canvas to start exploring that data. You can go to the sheets to begin your analysis. The Tableau workspace consists of a collection of sheets, menu bar, toolbar, shelves, and a lot of other elements. Sheets can be worksheets, dashboards, or stories.

The Data Pane

In the worksheet, you can see the column headers from your data shown as fields on the left side, in the data pane. Tableau automatically classifies the fields of each table or folder into two – Dimensions and Measures. Dimension fields appear above the grey line and measure fields appear below the grey line.

Dimensions hold qualitative data (discrete/categorical variables) such as name or date.
Measures hold quantitative numerical data (continuous variables) such as sales or profit.
Aggregate measures can also be created easily while working on the data.

If you have related dimension fields, sometimes you might want to group them in a folder, or as a hierarchy.
For example, the fields First Name, Middle name, and Last Name, can be grouped into a single hierarchy as Name. You can drill down into a hierarchy by clicking the + sign in a field or drill back up by clicking the - sign in a field.
If you have more than one data source in a workbook, click the data source connection name in the Data pane to select it for use.

Creating visualization

Data can be visualized as any sort of graph or chart. Visualization of the data can be created by dragging and dropping the required fields in the different shelves available.

Rows: Represents the x-axis of your graphs or charts
Columns: Represents the y-axis of your graphs or charts
Filter: Filters help you view a drilled down version of your data. You can view the data of any specific category as you wish.
Pages: Pages work on the same principle as Filters, with the difference that you can actually see the changes as you shift between the Paged values.
Marks: The Marks property is used to control the mark types of your data. You may choose to represent your data using different shapes, sizes, colours, or text. You can use this to add depth to your analysis.
Show Me: When you drag and drop fields into the visualization are, default graphs will be created by Tableau. You can change into any type of graph, by referring to the Show Me option available on the top right corner.

You can create different worksheets to create different views on the same or different data.

The Analytics Pane

In Tableau Desktop, options for adding Analytics objects to the view are available in the Analytics pane, which appears on the left side of the workspace, next to the Data pane. For example, reference lines and bands are available when you edit an axis, and trend lines and forecasts are available from the analysis menu. To use an item from the Analytics pane, drag it into the view. When you drag an item, Tableau will show the possible destinations for that item.The range of choices varies depending on the type of item and the current view.

Trend Line, available in the analytics pane, is the most commonly used analytics object to analyse the relationship between the fields. It also shows the mathematical expression for the correlation between the fields, the P-Value, and the R-Squared value. Trend Line Model types available in Tableau are: Linear, Logarithmic, Exponential, and Polynomial.

Types of Charts and Graphs

The following are some of the types of charts and graphs available in tableau:

Bar Chart - Bar charts are one of the most common data visualizations. You can use them to quickly compare data across categories, highlight differences, show trends and outliers, and reveal historical highs and lows at a glance.
Line Chart - The line chart, or line graph, connects several distinct data points, presenting them as one continuous evolution. Use line charts to view trends in data, usually over time.
Pie Chart - Pie charts are powerful for adding detail to other visualizations. Alone, a pie chart doesn’t give the viewer a way to compare information quickly and accurately.
Maps - Maps are a no-brainer for visualizing any kind of location information, whether it’s postal codes, state abbreviations, country names, or your own custom geocoding.
Density Maps - Density maps reveal patterns or relative concentrations that might otherwise be hidden due to an overlapping mark on a map—helping you identify locations with greater or fewer numbers of data points.
Scatter Plot - Scatter plots are an effective way to investigate the relationship between different variables, showing if one variable is a good predictor of another, or if they tend to change independently. The chart can then be enhanced with analytics like cluster analysis or trend lines.
Gantt Chart - Gantt charts display a project schedule or show changes in activity over time. A Gantt chart shows steps that need to be completed before others can begin, along with resource allocation.
Bubble Chart - Although bubbles aren’t technically their own type of visualization, using them as a technique adds detail to scatter plots or maps to show the relationship between three or more measures. Varying the size and colour of circles creates visually compelling charts that present large volumes of data at once.
Tree map – Tree maps relate different segments of your data to the whole. As the name of the chart suggests, each rectangle in a tree map is subdivided into smaller rectangles, or sub-branches, based on its proportion to the whole. They make efficient use of space to show percent total for each category.

Dashboards and Stories

A dashboard is a collection of several views, allowing you compare a variety of data simultaneously. For example, if you have a set of views that you want to review every day, you can create a dashboard by combining the views so that it displays all the views at once, rather than navigate to separate worksheets. Like worksheets, you access dashboards from tabs at the bottom of a workbook.

Data in both the sheets and the dashboards is connected; when you make any changes in a sheet, any dashboards containing it will change, and vice versa. Both sheets and dashboards update with the latest available data from the data source. You can as well add captions to your dashboards.

In Tableau, you can also create a story with a sequence of visualizations that together conveys some information. A story can be used to tell a data narrative, provide context, demonstrate how decisions relate to outcomes, or to simply make the visualizations more readable. A story is created as a separate sheet having a collection of several other sheets, brought together, and arranged in a sequence. Each individual sheet in a story is called as a story point.
